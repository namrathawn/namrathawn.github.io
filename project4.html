Project Overview

This project uses X‚Äëray Absorption Spectroscopy (XAS) to uncover how catalytic materials function at the atomic scale in energy conversion reactions such as hydrogen evolution reaction (HER) and oxygen reduction reaction (ORR). XAS allows the local structure and electronic state of specific elements in complex oxides and nanomaterials to be tracked while the catalyst is operating, revealing which sites are active, how they change under bias or illumination, and why some materials are more stable or selective than others.

X-ray Absorption Spectroscopy (XAS)

XAS is divided into two complementary regimes that probe different aspects of the local environment around an absorbing atom.

XANES: X-ray Absorption Near-Edge Spectroscopy

EXAFS: Extended X-ray Absorption Fine-Structure

XANES : transitions to unfilled bound states, nearly bound states, continuum - low energy photoelectrons
EXAFS : ~ 50 ‚Äì 1000 eV from edge, transitions to continuum ‚Äì high energy photoelectrons

Why XAS?

XAS is an element specific probe that measures how atoms absorb tunable X‚Äërays as a function of energy, providing direct information on oxidation state, coordination environment, and metal‚Äìligand bonding. Near‚Äëedge XANES features are sensitive to electronic structure and spin state, while the EXAFS region encodes quantitative details about coordination number and interatomic distances, giving complementary "electronic" and "geometric" views of the same active site. Together, XANES and EXAFS link atomic‚Äëscale structure to catalytic performance in situ and operando, making XAS a uniquely powerful tool for designing more active, durable catalysts.
