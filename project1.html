Green Hydrogen Production

Duration: 2023 - Present | Field: Green hydrogen, Photoelectrocatalysis

Project Overview

This project investigates functional transition metal oxides as scalable, earth-abundant materials for green hydrogen production via water splitting. The work centers on photoelectrocatalysis, where semiconductor electrodes harvest sunlight and drive the oxygen and hydrogen evolution reactions, converting solar energy directly into chemical fuel. By tailoring composition, defect chemistry, and nanostructure, the project aims to overcome current limits in efficiency, stability, and cost for solar-driven hydrogen technologies.

Research Goals

  • Design and synthesize novel transition metal oxide photoelectrodes tailored for efficient visible-light absorption and charge separation.
  • Optimize photoelectrochemical water splitting performance, targeting higher solar to hydrogen conversion efficiency and operational stability.
  • Elucidate structure–property relationships using advanced X-ray absorption spectroscopy and related techniques to link local electronic structure with catalytic activity.
  • Develop strategies to scale up catalyst synthesis and electrode fabrication toward processes compatible with industrial green hydrogen production.

Experimental Approach

The experimental strategy combines materials synthesis, advanced characterization, and photoelectrochemical testing. Transition metal oxides are prepared as thin films or nanostructured electrodes using sputtering, with controlled doping and morphology. Structural and electronic properties are probed using X-ray diffraction, electron microscopy, and spectroscopic techniques, while photoelectrochemical measurements (J–V curves, incident photon to current efficiency, stability tests) under simulated sunlight quantify activity and guide iterative materials optimization.

Collaborations

This work is conducted in collaboration with the Department of Physics and Astronomy at University of Padova, with additional support from international research facilities for synchrotron measurements.
